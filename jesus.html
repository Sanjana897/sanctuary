How Much of A “Sanctuary” is New York City, Really?

By TK

As a result of NAFTA leaving Mexico’s economy in ruins, Jesus became part of one of the largest waves of Mexican migration to the United States.

Leaving to the U.S became a trend in Jesús’ family. His parents followed the chain before he could even meet them. He was left in Puebla to the care of his grandmother, a local midwife in the village.

"Can be audiobox: My grandmother walked us over to the coyote and I’m thinking she’s gonna get in the car with us and she doesn’t. And I started bawling and crying."

“It was extremely devastating and extremely exciting at the same time” is how Jesús remembers the two-week trip to he took to Los Angeles nearly sixteen years ago. To get to California, the coyote provided fake documents for him to pass through security in the airport and board a flight to LA.

I had no idea when I crossed the border because. I just remember. I was in Tijuana one evening so I would go in the street. Everyone speaks Spanish. And then the next day I was in. Los Angeles. Everyone speaks English.

Meeting his parents was not what he expected - he suddenly saw himself having to share a one bedroom apartment with ten people and dealing with an abusive dad. The situation led Jesús to gain permanent residency through the Special Immigrant Juveniles (SIJ) status, which is a program that grants Green Card to foreign children who have been abused, abandoned, or neglected by a parent.

Although he obtained legal status, he still finds himself far from finding sanctuary. Much of his family and friends remain undocumented and daily face the threat of deportation. His siblings constantly report situations in which they feel scared to be forced to live the country.

Can be audioquote: In my experience just hearing from my siblings the threat of deportation as a way of disciplining you in the workplace. I mean that's just part of the culture of working as an undocumented immigrant that you've got to stay in line or else you know we'll call immigration.

With Trump’s election, the situation got worse. The vow of deporting many of the estimated 11 million immigrants in the U.S. and building a wall along the U.S - Mexico border increases the sentiment of unsafety for many of Jesus’ loved ones who still don’t hold legal status in the country. Recently, the president also attempted to cut federal funds from sanctuary cities that limit cooperation with immigration enforcement. Although a judge in San Francisco blocked the effort, Trump’s new policy puts many immigrants at risk of deportation for minor felonies.
